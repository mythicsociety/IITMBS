The Core Idea: Perfect Opposites

The most important thing to remember is that exponential and logarithmic functions are inverses. They are two sides of the same coin and exist to "undo" one another. This interactive guide will help you explore their properties and applications.

An exponential function tells you how big something gets after a certain amount of growth. For example, $2^3 = 8$ means doubling something for 3 steps results in 8.

A logarithmic function tells you how much time it took to grow to a certain size. For example, $\log_2(8) = 3$ asks "how many doubling steps does it take to get to 8?".

Logarithmic Functions: The "How Long?" Function

This function is the inverse of the exponential. It finds the exponent needed to reach a certain value. The general form is $f(x) = \log_a(x)$. Notice the vertical asymptote at x=0, which reinforces the rule that you can only take the log of a positive number.

The Laws of Logarithms

These three essential rules let you simplify and solve complex equations. Click on each tab to explore the rule and an example.

$\log_a(A \cdot B) = \log_a(A) + \log_a(B)$

Multiplying inside a log is the same as adding the logs outside.

Example: $\log_2(8 \cdot 16) = \log_2(128) = 7$
is the same as
$\log_2(8) + \log_2(16) = 3 + 4 = 7$

$\log_a(A / B) = \log_a(A) - \log_a(B)$

Dividing inside a log is the same as subtracting the logs outside.

Example: $\log_3(81 / 3) = \log_3(27) = 3$
is the same as
$\log_3(81) - \log_3(3) = 4 - 1 = 3$

$\log_a(A^n) = n \cdot \log_a(A)$

An exponent inside a log can be moved to the front as a multiplier. This is the most important rule for solving equations.

Example: $\log_{10}(100^3) = \log_{10}(1,000,000) = 6$
is the same as
$3 \cdot \log_{10}(100) = 3 \cdot 2 = 6$
