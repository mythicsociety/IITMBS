Interactive Topic Summary

The graphs of polynomial functions are always smooth and continuous. This means they do not have any sharp corners, cusps, gaps, or breaks. The overall direction of the graph on the far left and far right (its end behavior) is determined by the term with the highest power, known as the leading term.

Zeros (or roots) of a polynomial are the x-values for which the function's output is zero. Graphically, these are the x-intercepts. Zeros can be found by setting the polynomial equal to zero and solving, often by factoring, using the quadratic formula, or through graphical analysis. The Intermediate Value Theorem is a useful tool that states if a polynomial has values of opposite signs at two points, it must have at least one zero between those points.

The multiplicity of a zero refers to the number of times its corresponding factor appears in the factored form of the polynomial. The multiplicity affects the graph's behavior at the x-intercept:

  • Odd Multiplicity (1, 3, 5...): The graph crosses the x-axis at the zero.
  • Even Multiplicity (2, 4, 6...): The graph touches the x-axis (is tangent) and "bounces off".
Higher multiplicities cause the graph to appear flatter as it approaches and leaves the zero.

There is a direct relationship between a polynomial's degree and its graphical features:

  • A polynomial of degree 'n' can have at most 'n' real zeros.
  • A polynomial of degree 'n' can have at most 'n-1' turning points (where the graph changes from increasing to decreasing, or vice versa).

A limit describes the value that a function or sequence "approaches" as the input approaches some value. For a function $f(x)$ at a point $x=a$, we examine the behavior from both sides. The limit exists if and only if the left-hand limit and the right-hand limit both exist and are equal. Limits are fundamental for understanding continuity and derivatives.

A function is continuous at a point if its graph can be drawn through that point without lifting the pen. Formally, a function $f$ is continuous at a point $x=a$ if:

  1. $f(a)$ is defined.
  2. The limit of $f(x)$ as $x$ approaches $a$ exists.
  3. The limit equals the function's value: $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$.
All polynomial functions are continuous everywhere.
