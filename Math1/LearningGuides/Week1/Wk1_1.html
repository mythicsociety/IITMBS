Natural Numbers and Their Operations

Welcome! Let's start a journey to understand the very first numbers we ever learn about: natural numbers. Think of them as the building blocks for all of math.

What Are Natural Numbers?

Imagine you have a basket of apples. How many are there? One, two, three... The numbers you use to count things are called natural numbers. They are the simplest numbers we know: $0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5,$ and so on, forever!

What about zero (0)? The number 0 is super important! It represents "nothing" or "no objects." In this guide, we'll include 0 in our set of natural numbers, which we write with a special symbol: $\mathbb{N} = \{0, 1, 2, 3, ...\}$.

When we take away a bigger number from a smaller one, like $5 - 6$, we get a negative number. To handle this, we expand our number system to include negative numbers. This bigger group, $\{..., -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, ...\}$, is called the integers, written as $\mathbb{Z}$.

The Four Basic Operations

With numbers, we can do four basic things: add, subtract, multiply, and divide. Let's see what happens when we do these with natural numbers.

  • Addition (+): If you add two natural numbers, you always get another natural number. Example: $3 + 5 = 8$.
  • Subtraction (-): This is where it gets tricky! If you subtract a bigger number from a smaller one, like $4 - 9$, you get $-5$, which is an integer but not a natural number.
  • Multiplication (×): Multiplying two natural numbers always gives a natural number. Example: $4 \times 6 = 24$.
  • Division (÷): Sometimes division works out perfectly ($20 \div 5 = 4$), but other times it doesn't ($19 \div 5$ doesn't give a whole number).

Multiplication is Just Repeated Addition

What does $7 \times 4$ really mean? It means you have 4 groups of 7. So, it's just a shortcut for adding 7 to itself 4 times: $7 + 7 + 7 + 7 = 28$.

Powers are Repeated Multiplication

Just like multiplication is repeated addition, raising a number to a power (exponentiation) is repeated multiplication.

  • $m^2$ is called "$m$ squared" and means $m \times m$. It's called squared because it gives the area of a square with side length $m$.
  • $m^3$ is called "$m$ cubed" and means $m \times m \times m$. It's called cubed because it gives the volume of a cube with side length $m$.
  • $m^k$ is "$m$ to the power of $k$" and means multiplying $m$ by itself $k$ times.

Division and What's Left Over

Division is like sharing things equally. If you have 20 mangoes for 5 friends, each gets 4. This is because you can subtract 5 from 20 four times until you have nothing left.

But what if you have 19 mangoes for 5 friends? You can give each friend 3 mangoes, but you'll have 4 left over. You can't give out another round.

  • The number of full rounds you can do is the quotient. For $19 \div 5$, the quotient is 3.
  • The amount left over is the remainder. For $19 \div 5$, the remainder is 4.

Modulus Operator: In math and programming, we often use mod to find the remainder. So, we'd write $19 \text{ mod } 5 = 4$.

Factors and Prime Numbers

A number a is a factor of b if a divides b with no remainder. For example, the factors of 12 are 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, and 12. This is because $1 \times 12 = 12$, $2 \times 6 = 12$, and $3 \times 4 = 12$.

The Special Primes

Some numbers are special because they only have two factors: 1 and themselves. These are called prime numbers. The first few primes are 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, ...

Is 1 a prime number? No! It only has one factor (itself), but primes need to have exactly two different factors.

Prime Factorization

One of the coolest facts in math is that any natural number greater than 1 can be written as a unique product of prime numbers. It's like a secret code for each number!

For example, $126 = 2 \times 3 \times 3 \times 7$, which we can write as $2 \times 3^2 \times 7$. There's no other combination of primes that will multiply to 126.
