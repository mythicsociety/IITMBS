A Friendly Introduction to Graphs

Let's explore how we can represent relationships using a simple but powerful idea: the graph.

What is a Graph? (The Big Picture)

Forget the charts you drew in math class with x and y axes. In computer science, a graph is much simpler: it's just a way to show connections between things.

Imagine you have a bunch of items, and some of them are related to each other. A graph is just a picture of that. The items are called nodes (or sometimes vertices), and the connections between them are called edges.

Nodes are the things, edges are the relationships.

  • Nodes (Vertices): These are the individual items. In our examples, a node will be a student.
  • Edges: These are the lines that connect two nodes, representing a relationship. For example, an edge could mean "can mentor" or "is similar to".

That's it! A graph is just a set of dots and the lines connecting them. This simple idea helps us solve all sorts of interesting problems, like finding study groups or planning train journeys.

Directed Graphs: When Relationships Have a Direction

Sometimes, a relationship is a one-way street. For example, if Student A can mentor Student B, it doesn't automatically mean Student B can mentor Student A. This is where directed graphs come in. We use arrows instead of plain lines for the edges to show the direction of the relationship.

Example: The Mentoring Rule

A student A can mentor student B in a subject if:

A's mark is 10 to 20 points higher than B's mark in that same subject.

This rule creates a directed relationship. If A has 90 in Maths and B has 75, we can draw an arrow from A to B. We can even label the edge with "Maths" to show what the mentoring is for.

The arrow shows that A can mentor B, but not the other way around (in Maths).

This is called a directed edge. It has a source (the mentor) and a destination (the mentee). In this graph, an edge from A to B means "A can mentor B".

Paths & Cycles: Finding Study Groups

Once we have our directed mentoring graph, we can look for interesting patterns. Two important patterns are paths and cycles.

Paths: A Chain of Mentoring

A path is just a sequence of connected edges, following the arrows. For example, what if Student A can mentor Student B, and Student B can mentor Student C, all in the same subject (like Maths)?

This is a valid path! It's like a mentoring chain. For this to happen, their marks might be something like A=90, B=75, and C=60.

Cycles: Mutually Beneficial Study Groups

A cycle is a special kind of path that starts and ends at the same node. It's like a loop! This is the key to finding great study groups where everyone helps and gets help.

Is a cycle possible in a single subject? No! Because if A mentors B (A's marks > B's marks) and B mentors A (B's marks > A's marks), it's a contradiction. You can't have both!

But what if we use different subjects? Absolutely!

2-Person Study Group (Cycle of Length 2)

This is the simplest and most common study group. It's a perfect partnership!

  • A mentors B in Maths.
  • B mentors A in Physics.

3-Person Study Group (Cycle of Length 3)

We can even find larger groups where help flows in a circle.

  • A mentors B in Maths.
  • B mentors C in Physics.
  • C mentors A in Chemistry.

In this group, every student is both a mentor and a mentee. It's a perfectly balanced arrangement!

So, by building a graph of mentoring relationships, we can find these cycles to automatically form effective study groups.

Undirected Graphs: When Relationships are Mutual

What about relationships that are two-way streets? For example, if Student A is "similar" to Student B, then Student B is also "similar" to Student A. There's no direction here. For these cases, we use an undirected graph, where the edges are just simple lines without arrows.

Example: The Similarity Rule

Two students are similar if:

The absolute difference in their marks is less than 10 in ALL subjects (Maths AND Physics AND Chemistry).

This is a very strict condition. If two students meet this criterion, we can connect them with an undirected edge. It signifies a strong, mutual academic connection.

A simple line means the relationship is mutual. A is similar to B, and B is similar to A.

This kind of graph helps us find groups of students who are performing at a very comparable level across the board.

Cliques: Finding Tightly-Knit Groups of Similar Students

In our undirected "similarity" graph, a very special pattern we can look for is a clique (pronounced "kleek").

A clique is a group of nodes where every single node is connected to every other node in the group. Think of it as a group of best friends where everyone is friends with everyone else. In our case, it's a group of students who are all mutually similar to each other.

What does a Clique of 4 Students Look Like?

In this group of 4 students (A, B, C, D), A is similar to B, C, and D. B is similar to A, C, and D, and so on. It's a completely connected sub-group.

Finding cliques is useful for identifying clusters of students with very similar academic profiles. A large clique might indicate a group of students who are progressing together, whether they are high-achievers, average students, or students who are struggling as a group.

How Do We Represent Graphs in Code?

Drawing graphs on paper is great for understanding, but how does a computer work with them? One common way is using an Adjacency Matrix.

A matrix is just a grid or a table. If we have 'n' students, we can create an n-by-n grid. The cell at Matrix[i][j] tells us if there's an edge from student `i` to student `j`.

  • We put a 1 in `Matrix[i][j]` if an edge exists from `i` to `j`.
  • We put a 0 if there is no edge.

Here's the pseudocode from the lecture slides for creating the directed mentoring graph using this matrix idea:

Procedure CreateMentorGraph(marks)
  n = length(keys(marks))
  mentorGraph = CreateMatrix(n, n)  // Creates an n x n matrix of 0s

  foreach i in keys(marks) {
    foreach j in keys(marks) {
      ijMarksDiff = marks[i] - marks[j]

      if (10 <= ijMarksDiff and ijMarksDiff <= 20) {
        mentorGraph[i][j] = 1  // Set to 1 to represent the edge
      }
    }
  }
  return(mentorGraph)
End CreateMentorGraph

For an undirected graph like our similarity graph, the matrix will be symmetric. This means if `Matrix[i][j]` is 1, then `Matrix[j][i]` will also be 1, because the relationship is mutual.
