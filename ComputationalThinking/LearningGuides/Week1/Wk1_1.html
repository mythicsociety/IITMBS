Introduction to Computational Thinking

Welcome! This course is your first step into the world of programming, and it all starts with something called Computational Thinking. Before we can write code, we need to learn how to organize our thoughts in a systematic way, much like following a recipe to cook a meal.

A recipe gives you ingredients and a sequence of steps. Similarly, programming involves giving a computer a precise sequence of steps to achieve a goal. The amazing thing is that many different problems, from arranging books on a shelf to hanging clothes on a line, can be solved using the same underlying patterns of thinking. Our goal is to learn how to recognize and use these patterns.

To make these ideas concrete, we will be working with three simple, hands-on datasets throughout this course.

1. Our Example Datasets

To illustrate the patterns of computational thinking, we'll use three different sets of data, each represented as a collection of cards.

Dataset 1: Student Marks

This dataset consists of simplified student report cards. Each card contains specific pieces of information, or "fields," laid out in the same way on every card.

  • Fields: Name, Gender, Date of Birth, Town/City, Marks in Maths, Physics, and Chemistry, and a Total score.
  • Unique ID: Each card has a unique number on it so we can tell them apart, even if two students have the same name.

Dataset 2: Shopping Bills

Our second dataset represents shopping bills from different stores. Each bill is for a specific customer and lists the items they purchased.

  • Bill Fields: Shop Name, Customer Name, a unique Bill Number, and a Grand Total.
  • Item Fields: For each item on the bill, we have its Name, Type (e.g., Food, Toiletries), Quantity, Unit Price, and Total Cost.

Dataset 3: Paragraph Words

For our third dataset, we've taken a paragraph of text from a book and broken it down, putting each word onto its own card.

  • Fields: The Word itself, a unique Sequence Number (its position in the paragraph), its Part of Speech (e.g., noun, verb, pronoun), and the Number of Letters in the word.
