What is a List of Lists? ðŸ¤”

So far, we've thought about lists as simple, flat sequences of items, like a list of numbers or a list of words. But what if a list could hold other lists as its items? That's exactly what a list of lists is!

Think about how we read. A paragraph isn't just a jumble of words. It's a structured collection of sentences. Each sentence, in turn, is a collection of words. This is a perfect real-world example of a nested structure:

  • A paragraph can be a list of sentences.
  • Each sentence can be a list of words.
  • Each word can even be a list of letters.

This ability to nest lists inside other lists gives us a powerful way to organize and work with complex, hierarchical data. Instead of just having one long, flat sequence, we can create structures that better represent how things are related in the real world.

Understanding the Nested Structure ðŸ§±

When we work with a list of lists, we're dealing with different levels of data. Let's take the example of shopping data. Imagine we have a record of every item a customer buys.

A simple, flat list might just be a long sequence of purchases. But that's not very organized. A better way would be to group these purchases. We could say:

  • We have a main list of customers.
  • Each customer in that list has their own list of shopping trips (bills).
  • Each shopping trip has a list of items they bought.

This hierarchical structure is much more useful! If we want to analyze the shopping pattern of a specific customer, we can just grab their list of trips from the main list. This is much easier than searching through a giant, unorganized list of every purchase ever made. This is the power of using a list of lists to represent data that has a natural hierarchy.
