How We Articulate Sounds

In the last lesson, we learned *where* sounds are made (the places of articulation). Now, we'll focus on *how* they are made. This is called the manner of articulation. Understanding features like voicing and aspiration will give you a much deeper insight into the fine details of English pronunciation and help you pinpoint specific areas for improvement.

Manners of Articulation

If two sounds are made in the same place (e.g., /k/ and /g/ are both velar sounds), what makes them different? The answer lies in the manner of articulation. Let's look at two key features.

Voicing (Vocal Cord Vibration)

Inside your larynx (voice box), you have vocal cords. For some sounds, these cords vibrate, creating a buzzing or resonance. These are called voiced sounds. For other sounds, they don't vibrate. These are voiceless sounds.

Try this: Place your palm gently on your throat and say "ssssss" then "zzzzzz". You should feel a vibration for "zzzzzz" but not for "ssssss".

  • Voiceless: /p/, /t/, /k/, /s/, /f/
  • Voiced: /b/, /d/, /g/, /z/, /v/

Aspiration (A Puff of Air)

Aspiration refers to the small, extra puff of air that follows certain voiceless consonants. These are aspirated sounds. Sounds without this puff are unaspirated.

Try this: Hold your hand in front of your mouth. Say "pin". You should feel a strong puff of air on the 'p'. Now say "spin". The 'p' sound here has almost no puff of air.

  • Aspirated: The 'p' in pin, 't' in top, 'k' in king.
  • Unaspirated: The 'p' in spin, 't' in stop, 'k' in skin.

Comparing Sound Systems: English vs. Indian Languages

Many speakers of Indian languages use sounds that are very close to, but not exactly the same as, sounds in English. Recognizing these differences is a huge step toward clearer pronunciation.

Alveolar /t/ vs. Retroflex /T/

This is one of the most common differences. Many Indian languages (both Dravidian and Indo-Aryan) have retroflex sounds, but English does not.

  • Retroflex /T/, /D/ (e.g., in Hindi): To make this sound, the tip of your tongue curls or folds backward and then flaps forward to hit the alveolar ridge.
  • Alveolar /t/, /d/ (in English): To make this sound, the tip of your tongue simply touches the alveolar ridge without curling back. It's a much lighter, simpler movement.

Labiodental /f/ vs. Bilabial /pʰ/

Another key difference involves the /f/ sound.

  • Bilabial /pʰ/ (e.g., the sound in Hindi फल 'phal'): This is an aspirated 'p' sound made with both lips coming together, followed by a puff of air.
  • Labiodental /f/ (in English): This sound is a fricative, made by touching your upper teeth to your lower lip and letting air flow through. Words like fan, friend, and fat all use this sound.

Why Accents Exist: The Science of "Indian English"

Have you ever wondered why it's hard to change an accent? There's a scientific reason for it. The term "Indian English" isn't offensive; it's a linguistic term for the variety of English spoken in India, just like "American English" or "British English".

  • Vocal Tract Maturity: Before the age of 12, our vocal tract (the muscles in our mouth and throat) matures and becomes conditioned to the specific sounds of our native language(s).
  • Sound System Transfer: Because our muscles are "trained" for our first language, we naturally tend to use those familiar sounds when we speak a new language. This is why a speaker of Hindi might use a retroflex /T/ instead of an alveolar /t/ in English—it's the closest sound their mouth is used to making.
  • Practice Makes Progress: While it's difficult to completely change this conditioning, it is absolutely possible to learn and practice new sounds like the English alveolar /t/ or labiodental /f/. Knowing the difference is the first and most important step!
