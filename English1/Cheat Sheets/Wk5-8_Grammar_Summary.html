Verb Tenses: Past, Present & Future

Past Tense

  • Simple Past: Describes a finished event in the past (e.g., "She returned yesterday.").
  • Past Progressive: An ongoing action in the past, often interrupted (e.g., "He was reading when the phone rang.").
  • Past Perfect: A 'past before the past'; an action completed before another past action (e.g., "She had finished the report before the meeting started.").
  • Past Perfect Progressive: Emphasizes the duration of a past action before another past action (e.g., "They had been waiting for an hour.").

Present Tense

  • Simple Present: For habitual actions, general truths, and states (e.g., "He reads the newspaper every morning.").
  • Present Progressive: An action in progress now (e.g., "I am looking for an intern.").
  • Present Perfect: A past action with relevance to the present (e.g., "The train has finally arrived."). Often used with 'for', 'since', 'already', 'yet'.
  • Present Perfect Progressive: An action that started in the past and continues to the present (e.g., "The baby has been crying for an hour.").

Future Tense

  • Simple Future (will): For predictions and spontaneous decisions (e.g., "The train will arrive in 5 minutes.").
  • Future Progressive: An action that will be in progress at a specific future time (e.g., "This time tomorrow, I will be traveling.").
  • Future Perfect: An action that will be completed before another future time (e.g., "By next year, she will have completed her degree.").
  • Future Perfect Progressive: Emphasizes the duration of an action up to a certain point in the future (e.g., "In June, I will have been living here for ten years.").

Subject-Verb Agreement

The verb in a sentence must agree with its subject in number (singular or plural).

  • Compound Subjects (and): Use a plural verb (e.g., "Annie and her brothers are at school.").
  • Compound Subjects (or/nor): The verb agrees with the subject closer to it (e.g., "The dog or the cats are outside." vs. "The cats or the dog is outside.").
  • Indefinite Pronouns: Words like 'each', 'everybody', 'anyone', 'no one' are singular (e.g., "Everybody knows Mr. Ram.").
  • Collective Nouns: Words like 'team', 'committee', 'family' are typically treated as singular (e.g., "The team runs during practice.").
  • Nouns ending in -s: Some nouns like 'news', 'mathematics', and 'politics' are singular (e.g., "The news is on at six.").
  • Paired Nouns: Nouns like 'scissors', 'trousers', 'glasses' are plural (e.g., "These scissors are dull.").
  • Phrases in Between: The verb agrees with the subject, not a noun in a phrase between them (e.g., "The woman with all the dogs walks down my street.").

Reading Skills

Fundamental Techniques

  • Skimming: Reading quickly to get a general overview or main idea. Focus on headings, subheadings, and the first sentences of paragraphs.
  • Scanning: Looking for specific keywords or information in a text without reading everything.

Comprehension Methods

  • SQ3R: A five-step method: Survey, Question, Read, Recite, Review.
  • PQRST: A similar five-step method: Preview, Question, Read, State/Self-recite, Test.

Critical Reading

  • Separating Fact from Opinion: Distinguishing between provable information and the author's beliefs or judgments.
  • Detecting Propaganda: Recognizing biased or misleading information used to promote a cause.
  • Recognizing Errors in Reasoning: Identifying logical fallacies (e.g., circular reasoning, false cause) that weaken an argument.

Listening Skills

Active Listening

A conscious effort to hear and understand the complete message being communicated. It involves:

  • Full concentration and removing distractions.
  • Providing feedback (nodding, verbal affirmations).
  • Asking clarifying questions.
  • Listening without assumptions or judgment.
  • Observing body language.

Passive Listening

Hearing without the intention of responding or retaining the information. Often occurs due to:

  • Disinterest in the topic.
  • Distractions (physical or mental).
  • Preoccupation with one's own thoughts.
  • Speaker's presentation style is not engaging.

Barriers to Effective Listening

  • Physical Distractions: External noise, uncomfortable environment.
  • Psychological Barriers: Assumptions, biases, judging the speaker.
  • Physiological Barriers: Hearing impairment, fatigue, hunger.

Phonology: Sounds, Syllables & Stress

Syllables & Stress

  • A syllable is a unit of pronunciation having one vowel sound.
  • Stress is the emphasis placed on a particular syllable in a word.
  • Stress patterns can change a word's meaning or function (e.g., 'REcord' (noun) vs. 'reCORD' (verb)).
  • General Rule for 2-Syllable Words: Nouns and adjectives often have stress on the first syllable; verbs often have stress on the second.

Aspiration

Aspiration is the strong puff of air that accompanies the release of some consonants.

  • Voiceless stops /p/, /t/, /k/ are aspirated at the beginning of a stressed syllable.
  • Example: The 'p' in 'pot' is aspirated, but the 'p' in 'spot' is not.

Plural Markers

The pronunciation of the plural '-s' ending depends on the final sound of the word.

  • /s/ sound: After voiceless sounds like /p/, /t/, /k/ (e.g., cats).
  • /z/ sound: After voiced sounds like /b/, /d/, /g/, /v/ and vowels (e.g., dogs, trees).
  • /ɪz/ sound: After sibilant sounds like /s/, /z/, /ʃ/, /tʃ/ (e.g., buses, judges).
