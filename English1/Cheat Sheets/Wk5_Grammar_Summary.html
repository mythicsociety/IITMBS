English 1: Grammar Summary

Week 5: Tenses & Subject-Verb Agreement

Verb Tenses

+

Past Tenses

Simple Past

A completed action at a specific point in the past.

Example: She returned from the conference yesterday.

Past Progressive

An ongoing action in the past (often interrupted).

Example: The girls were playing when it started to rain.

Past Perfect

An action completed before another past action ("past before past").

Example: She realized she had left her keys at the restaurant.

Past Perfect Progressive

Emphasizes the duration of an ongoing action before another past action.

Example: They had been conducting research for a year before the launch.

Present Tenses

Simple Present

A fact, habit, or permanent state.

Example: Our lead programmer speaks Python fluently.

Present Progressive

An action happening right now.

Example: Look! The CEO is visiting our department.

Present Perfect

A past action with relevance or result in the present.

Example: Ravi has not been to Europe.

Present Perfect Progressive

An action that started in the past and continues to the present.

Example: She has been working since morning.

Future Tenses

Simple Future

A prediction or future intention.

Example: The package will arrive tomorrow.

Future Progressive

An action in progress at a specific future time.

Example: This time next week, we will be relaxing on a beach.

Future Perfect

An action that will be completed before another future time.

Example: By 7:00 am, will you have arrived?

Future Perfect Progressive

Emphasizes the duration of an action up to a specific future time.

Example: In September, she will have been working here for a decade.

Subject-Verb Agreement

+

Standard Rules

  • Subjects joined by and are plural: Annie and her brothers are at school.
  • Singular subjects joined by or/nor are singular: The dog or the cat is outside.
  • For subjects joined by or/nor, the verb agrees with the closer subject (Proximity Rule): Neither the manager nor the developers are able.
  • Intervening phrases (like along with, as well as) do not affect the verb. The verb agrees with the main subject: The politician, along with the reporters, is expected.
  • Indefinite pronouns (each, everybody, no one) are singular: Everybody is excited.

Complex & Irregular Cases

  • Collective nouns (team, family, flock) are usually singular: The team remains confident.
  • Nouns that look plural but are singular (news, mathematics) take a singular verb: The news is not good.
  • Some nouns are always plural (scissors, pants, trousers): Your pants were in the laundromat.
  • When preceded by "pair of", these nouns become singular: My pair of scissors needs a new blade.
  • Amounts of money, time, or distance are treated as a single unit: Ten dollars is not a lot.
