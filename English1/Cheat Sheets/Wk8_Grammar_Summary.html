Syllabification is the process of dividing a word into its constituent syllables. A syllable is a single, unbroken unit of pronunciation that must contain one vowel sound.

Key Principles:

  • Every syllable has exactly one vowel sound (the nucleus).
  • A syllable can be a single vowel sound or be surrounded by consonants.
  • The number of syllables in a word is determined by the number of vowel sounds you hear when pronouncing it.

Examples:

One Syllable: cat, run, strength Two Syllables: wa-ter, po-lice, hap-py Three Syllables: de-part-ment, mos-qui-to Four+ Syllables: in-for-ma-tion, un-be-liev-a-ble

Word Stress is the emphasis placed on a particular syllable within a multi-syllable word. The stressed syllable is pronounced longer, louder, and often at a higher pitch.

Common Stress Patterns:

  • Two-Syllable Nouns & Adjectives: Stress usually on the first syllable (e.g., PRE-sent, HAP-py).
  • Two-Syllable Verbs: Stress usually on the second syllable (e.g., pre-SENT, re-CEIVE).
  • Compound Nouns: Stress on the first part (e.g., TOOTH-paste, SEA-food).
  • Words ending in -tion, -ic: Stress on the syllable before the ending (e.g., edu-CA-tion, stra-TE-gic).
  • Words ending in -ee, -ique: Stress on the final syllable (e.g., guaran-TEE, u-NIQUE).

Aspiration refers to the audible puff of air that accompanies the pronunciation of voiceless stop consonants: /p/, /t/, and /k/.

Rules of Aspiration:

  • Aspirated [pʰ, tʰ, kʰ]: Occurs at the beginning of a word or a stressed syllable.
    • Examples: pot, top, key, attend, apply
  • Unaspirated [p, t, k]: Occurs when the sound follows an /s/ sound, or when it is in an unstressed syllable.
    • Examples: spot, stop, sky, baker, happy

The sound of the plural marker (-s, -es) in English depends on the final sound of the singular noun.

The Three Plural Sounds:

  • /s/ sound: Used after voiceless consonants (except sibilants).
    Examples: cats, books, laptops, myths
  • /z/ sound: Used after voiced consonants (except sibilants) and all vowel sounds.
    Examples: dogs, beds, keys, pencils
  • /ɪz/ or /əz/ sound: Used after sibilant sounds (/s/, /z/, /ʃ/, /ʒ/, /tʃ/, /dʒ/).
    Examples: boxes, wishes, churches, judges
